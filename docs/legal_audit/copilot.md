🧭 Core Legal Positioning You Must Make Crystal Clear

Your biggest legal risk is being mistaken for a retailer instead of a platform. EU consumer law is extremely strict for B2C sellers — but you are C2C. That means:
You MUST explicitly state:

    All sellers are private individuals, not businesses.

    Second Turn Games is not a seller, merchant, or reseller.

    You do not own, inspect, or guarantee the items.

    You only provide listing, payment, and logistics facilitation.

    Consumer protection rules for business sellers (14‑day withdrawal, warranties, etc.) do NOT apply.

If this is not extremely explicit, regulators may treat you as a “de facto seller,” which would impose:

    mandatory returns,

    2‑year warranty obligations,

    liability for product defects,

    full GDPR controller obligations for seller–buyer communication.

This is the #1 area where marketplaces accidentally accumulate legal debt.
🧩 Key Legal Areas You Need to Cover (Based on Your Model)
1. Platform Liability & Role Clarification

Your Terms should include:

    You are a hosting provider under the EU eCommerce Directive / Digital Services Act (DSA).

    You are not a party to the contract between buyer and seller.

    You are not responsible for item quality, legality, or description accuracy.

    You may remove listings that violate law or your policies.

    You provide payment and shipping facilitation only.

2. Digital Services Act (DSA) Compliance

As an online marketplace in the EU, you must comply with DSA obligations:

    Clear notice‑and‑action mechanism for illegal content (e.g., counterfeit games).

    Transparency about content moderation rules.

    Annual transparency reporting (even a simple one).

    A way for users to appeal moderation decisions.

    Identification of traders (if any sellers are actually businesses).

Since your users are private individuals, you must explicitly state that sellers must not misrepresent themselves as businesses.
3. Payments via EveryPay (Swedbank)

You need:

    A clear explanation that payments are processed by EveryPay, not by you.

    A statement that you do not store card details.

    A description of how disputes/chargebacks are handled.

    Clarification of when funds are released to sellers.

4. Shipping via Unisend SIA

You should specify:

    You act only as a facilitator for shipping label creation.

    Liability for lost/damaged packages follows Unisend’s terms, not yours.

    Sellers must package items appropriately.

    Buyers must inspect packages upon receipt.

5. C2C Consumer Rights Clarification

Because your sellers are private persons:

    No 14‑day right of withdrawal.

    No statutory warranty obligations.

    No obligation for sellers to accept returns unless they choose to.

Your Terms must explicitly state this to avoid consumer‑protection regulators treating you as a B2C seller.
6. Dispute Resolution

You should define:

    Buyer–seller disputes are between the parties, not with you.

    You may assist but are not obligated to mediate.

    You may withhold payouts if fraud is suspected.

    You may suspend accounts for repeated disputes or rule violations.

7. Prohibited Items & Legality

You should include:

    A list of prohibited items (counterfeits, stolen goods, adult content, etc.).

    A requirement that sellers own the items they list.

    A requirement that items comply with local laws (e.g., no gambling items).

8. GDPR & Privacy Policy Adjustments

Because you operate in the EU and process payments + shipping, your Privacy Policy must clearly define:
You are the data controller for:

    Platform accounts

    Listing data

    Payment initiation metadata

    Shipping label creation

    Fraud prevention

EveryPay and Unisend are data processors or independent controllers depending on integration:

    If they process data strictly on your instructions → processors.

    If they determine their own purposes (likely for payments) → independent controllers.

You must:

    Identify each partner and their role.

    Provide legal bases for processing (contract, legitimate interest, legal obligation).

    Explain cross‑border transfers (if any).

    Provide retention periods.

    Provide user rights (access, deletion, portability, etc.).

9. Seller Terms Should Include

    Seller obligations to describe items accurately.

    Seller responsibility for legality and ownership.

    Seller responsibility for packaging and shipping.

    Seller responsibility for resolving buyer complaints.

    Commission/fees and payout rules.

    Fraud prevention and penalties.

    Right to suspend or terminate accounts.

10. Tax Clarification

Since sellers are private individuals:

    You should state that sellers are responsible for declaring any income.

    You do not provide tax reporting unless required by law.

    You may be required to report seller income under DAC7 (EU tax transparency rules).

DAC7 applies to marketplaces starting 2023 — you likely must:

    Collect seller identity information.

    Report annual seller earnings to tax authorities.

If your policies do not mention DAC7, that is a major compliance gap.
