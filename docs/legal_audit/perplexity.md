Business model

Based on the public site and privacy policy, Second Turn is a regional marketplace where users create listings, communicate with one another, select shipping, and in “Claim this game” flows you receive the buyer’s payment as the seller’s authorized commercial agent before routing funds onward.
That means your legal docs should not read like a simple bulletin-board platform if, operationally, you also intermediate payment, generate shipping labels, store transaction records, collect seller payout data, and run compliance checks tied to DAC7 thresholds.
A good legal package for this model usually separates three relationships cleanly: platform-to-user rules, seller-to-buyer sale terms, and the platform’s agency/payment/shipping role in between.

Seller status

Your privacy policy says sellers declare that they are private individuals, while also anticipating date of birth, address, TIN, IBAN, and DAC7-related verification when thresholds are approached.
That is a strong start, but the Seller Terms should go further and give you contractual rights to reclassify a seller, require additional information, pause listings or payouts for non-cooperation, and terminate accounts for false “private seller” declarations.
This matters because mandatory consumer-protection rules vary across EU member states and weak onboarding or misclassification can leave the platform exposed if the documents do not clearly say who must comply with which sales obligations.

    Spell out what a seller represents when choosing “private individual,” and state that false declarations can lead to suspension, withholding of payouts where legally permitted, and back-charging of resulting losses or claims.

    Mirror the DAC7 narrative from the privacy policy inside the Seller Terms, including when information must be supplied, what happens if it is missing, and that reporting cannot be opted out of once legally required.

    Include a process for repeated or high-volume sellers whose activity starts to look professional, because the current public text focuses on private sellers and threshold-based tax reporting.

Privacy issues

The privacy policy already does many things well: it identifies the controller, lists data categories, states GDPR legal bases, names key processors, gives retention periods, describes DAC7 reporting, and explains user rights.
The main improvements I would suggest are more precision and cross-document consistency, not a rewrite.

    Privacy says cookies are used and refers users to a Cookie Policy, so make sure that separate document is complete, easy to find, and fully consistent with the analytics-consent language in the privacy policy.

    Expand the international-transfer section by mapping each non-EU or US-linked provider to the relevant transfer mechanism, because the current policy states that some providers are US-based and rely on EU-US transfer frameworks and standard contractual clauses without much provider-level detail.

    Tighten retention logic for messages, listings, and dispute evidence, because “until deleted by the user or upon account deletion” may be too open-ended unless your Terms also explain when content is retained longer for fraud, complaints, tax, or legal claims.

    Clarify when buyer and seller phone numbers are merely operational for parcel notifications versus stored for future use, and make that choice flow consistent across checkout UX, Terms, and privacy disclosures.

Priority order

I would address the debt in this order: first, align Terms and Seller Terms with the “commercial agent” payment structure; second, tighten seller-status, payout-hold, and DAC7 clauses; third, specify shipping/refund/dispute mechanics; fourth, harmonize privacy, cookies, retention, and transfer disclosures.
